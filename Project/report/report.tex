\section*{Introduction}

The New York City Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) publishes monthly trip records for four for-hire vehicle categories: \textbf{Yellow taxi} (medallion cabs, primarily Manhattan), \textbf{Green taxi} (boro cabs, outer boroughs from 2013), \textbf{FHV} (for-hire vehicles dispatched through licensed bases), and \textbf{FHVHV} (high-volume FHV -- Uber and Lyft, reporting separately from February 2019 onwards) \cite{tlc2024data}. Together the four datasets span 2009--2026 with approximately \textbf{3.7 billion} trip records.

\textbf{Platform.} All processing was performed on \textbf{Snowflake} \cite{snowflake2024} in place of an HPC cluster, using a three-layer medallion architecture: \texttt{BRONZE} (raw staged Parquet files), \texttt{SILVER} (unified and cleaned tables), and \texttt{GOLD} (aggregations and enriched outputs). Large-scale transformations used Snowflake SQL; visualizations, format benchmarking, and external API fetching used Python in Snowpark. All code is under \texttt{scripts/t0/}--\texttt{scripts/t9/}.

%------------------------------------------------

\section*{T0 \& T1 -- Data Ingestion and Schema Unification}

\subsection*{T0: Downloading and Staging}

The Arnes HPC cluster held TLC data only up to January 2025. Missing monthly Parquet files (up to February 2026) were downloaded via parallel HTTP requests (\path{scripts/t0/missing_data.py}) and staged on Snowflake using the Snowpark connector (\path{scripts/t0/snowflake.py}). Four internal \texttt{BRONZE} stages were created: one per dataset type.

Schema analysis used a map-reduce pattern against Snowflake's \path{INFORMATION_SCHEMA}: the map step computed each file's column schema; the reduce step produced a union across all files. Results were stored in \path{BRONZE.T0_SCHEMA_ALL}. Key findings: column names varied between vintages (\texttt{airport\_fee} vs.\ \texttt{Airport\_fee}), types varied (vendor ID as integer or float), and new columns appeared in later years (e.g.\ \texttt{cbd\_congestion\_fee} from 2024).

\subsection*{T1: Silver Layer -- Schema Unification}

Four unified tables were created in \texttt{SILVER} (\path{scripts/t1/t1.sql}), clustered by \texttt{(PICKUP\_YEAR,\allowbreak{} PICKUP\_MONTH)} for efficient partition pruning. Type inconsistencies were resolved with \texttt{TRY\_TO\_DOUBLE}/\texttt{TRY\_TO\_NUMBER}; naming aliases were resolved with \texttt{COALESCE} across all candidate columns.

\section*{T2 -- Data Quality Analysis}

\subsection*{Methodology}

Quality checks were implemented in \path{scripts/t2/t2.sql} using a map-reduce pattern: the map step computed issue counts per \texttt{(SOURCE\_FILE, PICKUP\_YEAR)}; the reduce step aggregated by year. Checks applied (where columns existed):

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Unexpected year}: outside expected range per dataset.
  \item \textbf{Pickup outside source month}: $>$1 day outside the file's calendar month.
  \item \textbf{Pickup equals dropoff}: identical timestamps.
  \item \textbf{Dropoff before pickup}: reversed timestamps.
  \item \textbf{Zero distance}: trip distance or miles equal to zero.
  \item \textbf{Non-positive passenger count}: $\leq 0$ (Yellow/Green only).
  \item \textbf{Negative fare / total amount}.
\end{itemize}

FHV also checked missing location and dispatching base. FHVHV also checked non-positive trip time and negative driver pay.

\subsection*{Results -- Yellow and Green}

Table~\ref{tab:quality_yellow} shows selected-year quality metrics for Yellow taxi, and Figure~\ref{fig:quality_yg} shows the full trend for both Yellow and Green. Trips outside the valid year range are negligible (a few thousand). \textbf{Zero-distance rate grew from 0.56\% (2012) to 2.88\% (2025)}, likely reflecting app-cancelled trips. Most notably, \textbf{negative fare rose from near-zero before 2020 to 5.85\% in 2025}, consistent with growing digital refund entries. Green taxi shows the same trends but concentrated in outer-borough zones.

\subsection*{Results -- FHV and FHVHV}

FHV has the most severe issues (Figure~\ref{fig:quality_ff}). In 2015 and 2016, \textbf{virtually 100\% of records have dropoff before pickup} -- over 63M rows in 2015 and 132M in 2016 -- a systematic failure in early dispatching base reporting. \textbf{Missing location data affects 27\% of pickups and 98\% of dropoffs in 2015}; even in 2023--2025, over 75\% of FHV pickups lack a location ID.

\subsection*{Clean Views}

Four clean views were created in \texttt{SILVER}. Yellow and Green apply strict filters (valid year, pickup within source month, dropoff after pickup, distance $>$0, non-negative fare, passenger count null or $>$0). FHVHV applies equivalent strict filters. \textbf{FHV applies only a light filter} (valid year, non-empty base) to preserve the large fraction of early records with known systematic issues; downstream analyses work around the known problems explicitly.

\section*{T4 -- Storage Format Comparison}

\subsection*{Experimental Setup}

Benchmark target: the Green 2024 partition from \path{SILVER.GREEN_TRIPS_CLEAN} (\texttt{PICKUP\_YEAR=2024}), yielding \textbf{617,885 rows, 24 columns}. Four formats were benchmarked: Parquet/snappy (PyArrow \cite{arrow2024}), plain CSV (pandas), gzip CSV (pandas), and HDF5/gzip (h5py). Snowflake natively supports Parquet, CSV, and gzip CSV via \texttt{COPY INTO} stage exports; HDF5 was Python-only.

\subsection*{Measurement Methodology}

\textit{Python}: write time = single run; read time = median of 3 runs from disk. HDF5 used h5py with gzip compression for numeric columns and fixed-length byte strings for text/datetime.

\textit{Snowflake}: write time = duration of \texttt{COPY INTO} query (from \path{INFORMATION_SCHEMA}); read time = duration of \texttt{COUNT(*)} on the staged files, which forces full decompression and parse of every file. \texttt{SELECT *} was not used because staged CSV files carry no schema metadata and require explicit positional column references; \texttt{COUNT(*)} was used consistently across all formats.

\subsection*{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:formats} and Figure~\ref{fig:formats} show the benchmark results.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Format benchmark -- Green 2024, 617,885 rows. Bold = best per column.}
\label{tab:formats}
\small
\begin{tabular}{p{3.2cm}rrr}
\toprule
Format & Size (MB) & Read (s) & Write (s) \\
\midrule
Parquet [Python]    & 15.1 & \textbf{0.11} & 0.82 \\
CSV [Python]        & 90.4 & 1.21 & 6.84 \\
CSV.gz [Python]     & \textbf{13.2} & 1.40 & 18.48 \\
HDF5 [Python]       & 38.9 & 0.30 & \textbf{0.95} \\
Parquet [Snowflake] & 14.4 & 0.42 & 2.12 \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{figure*}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{t4/grapf_comparison.png}
\caption{Format benchmark: file size, read speed, and write speed for all format/engine combinations.}
\label{fig:formats}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{Parquet (snappy)} is the clear winner: \textbf{0.11 s read} ($11\times$ faster than plain CSV), 15.1 MB, and 0.82 s write. Columnar layout enables column-skipping; snappy balances compression ratio and CPU cost.

\textbf{CSV (gzip)} achieves the smallest size (13.2 MB) but write is \textbf{18.5 s} ($22\times$ slower than Parquet) due to single-threaded gzip. Suitable only for archival.

\textbf{Plain CSV} is strictly dominated: \textbf{6$\times$ larger} than Parquet with no speed advantage. Only advantage is universal tool compatibility.

\textbf{HDF5} (Python-only): 38.9 MB, 0.30 s read, 0.95 s write -- a viable middle ground, but unsupported in Snowflake and requires complex read code.

\textbf{Snowflake} read times (0.42--0.60 s) are slower than Python due to query planning and distributed execution overhead, but the \textbf{relative ordering is identical} to Python (Parquet fastest, gzip CSV slowest). Snowflake write includes encryption and object-store upload, making it 2--4$\times$ slower than local Python writes.

\section*{T5 -- Data Augmentation}

Each cleaned trip table was enriched with four external data sources: weather, school density, attraction density, and active event count. Final enriched tables are \path{GOLD.T5_YELLOW}, \path{GOLD.T5_GREEN}, \path{GOLD.T5_FHV}, and \path{GOLD.T5_FHVHV}. All data was fetched inside Snowflake via Python stored procedures using the \path{ALLOW_ALL_EAI} external network access integration (\path{scripts/t5/t5_fetch_data.sql}).

\subsection*{Taxi Zone Reference Tables}

Two zone tables underpin all spatial joins:

\textbf{\path{EXTERNAL_DATA.TAXI_ZONE_LOOKUP}}: The standard TLC taxi zone lookup CSV (downloaded directly from the TLC website). Provides \texttt{LOCATION\_ID}, \texttt{BOROUGH}, \texttt{ZONE}, and \texttt{SERVICE\_ZONE} for all 263 NYC zones.

\textbf{\path{EXTERNAL_DATA.TAXI_ZONES_GEOM}}: Geometry table for point-in-polygon joins. The TLC shapefile was reprojected from EPSG:2263 to EPSG:4326 using GeoPandas (\path{scripts/t5/convert_taxi.ipynb}), serialised to WKT, and loaded into Snowflake as a \texttt{GEOGRAPHY} column via \texttt{TO\_GEOGRAPHY()}.

\subsection*{External Data Sources}

\textbf{Weather} \cite{openmeteo2024}: Daily max/min temperature and precipitation for NYC (40.7831°N, $-$73.9712°W) fetched from Open-Meteo in five 3-year chunks. Joined to trips by pickup date.

\textbf{Schools}: NYC Open Data dataset \texttt{n3p6-zve2} (2019--20 School Locations). Up to 50,000 records fetched via Socrata API; coordinates extracted from nested GeoJSON. Spatially joined to zones with \texttt{ST\_CONTAINS()} to produce a \textbf{school count per zone} (\path{EXTERNAL_DATA.T5_SCHOOLS_BY_ZONE}).

\textbf{Attractions}: NYC Open Data dataset \texttt{fn6f-htvy} (Museums). Same pattern as schools; joined to produce \textbf{attraction count per zone} (\path{EXTERNAL_DATA.T5_ATTRACTIONS_BY_ZONE}).

\textbf{Events} \cite{nycopen2024}: NYC Open Data dataset \texttt{bkfu-528j} (Permitted Events, 2020--2024). Fetched in quarterly chunks (16 requests) to stay within the 10,000-row API limit. Each event was expanded across its active hours using \texttt{ARRAY\_GENERATE\_RANGE} and \texttt{LATERAL FLATTEN} in \path{scripts/t5/t5.sql}; events without an end time assume a 3-hour default. Result: \textbf{active event count per (hour, borough)}, joined to trips by \texttt{DATE\_TRUNC('hour', PICKUP\_DATETIME)}.

\subsection*{Final Enriched Tables}

Gold joins (\path{scripts/t5/t5_gold.sql}) temporarily scale the warehouse to \texttt{LARGE} to handle base tables up to 1.52B rows. Each output row adds: \texttt{BOROUGH}, \texttt{ZONE}, \texttt{SERVICE\_ZONE}, \texttt{TEMP\_MAX\_C}, \texttt{TEMP\_MIN\_C}, \texttt{PRECIPITATION\_MM}, \texttt{SCHOOL\_COUNT}, \texttt{ATTRACTION\_COUNT}, \texttt{ACTIVE\_EVENTS}.

\section*{T8 -- Impact of FHVHV on Traditional Services}

\subsection*{Regulatory Context}

Before February 2019, Uber and Lyft reported under the FHV dataset. The FHVHV regulation required operators dispatching $>$10,000 trips/day to obtain an HVFHS licence and report under the new FHVHV category. This caused an immediate structural break: \textbf{FHV monthly counts dropped from $\sim$24M (Jan 2019) to $\sim$1--2M}, while \textbf{FHVHV emerged at $\sim$18--20M/month}. All longitudinal analysis must account for this break. Juno (\texttt{HV0002}) and Via (\texttt{HV0004}) appeared briefly in FHVHV data ($<$2\% share) before exiting the market in 2019.

\subsection*{Absolute Trip Volumes}

\textbf{Yellow} peaked at $\sim$\textbf{170M trips/year} (2012--2013) -- effectively 100\% market share. By 2018 it had fallen to $\sim$82M ($-$52\%) and by 2023--2024 to $\sim$40M (\textbf{$-$76\% from peak}). \textbf{Green} never exceeded $\sim$20M/year and has now fallen to near zero. After the 2019 transition, \textbf{Uber settled at $\sim$160--180M/year} and \textbf{Lyft at $\sim$50--65M/year}.

\subsection*{Market Share Evolution}

Figure~\ref{fig:market} shows monthly market share over time. Yellow held $\sim$100\% in 2012, fell below 50\% by mid-2016, and reached $\sim$22\% just before the FHVHV transition. After February 2019, \textbf{Uber immediately captured $\sim$50\%} of all tracked trips, stabilising at \textbf{55--60\%}. Lyft holds a steady \textbf{$\sim$20--22\%}. Yellow has settled at \textbf{$\sim$13--15\%} since 2021; Green is \textbf{below 1\%}.

\section*{Conclusion}

We implemented a complete big data pipeline for \textbf{3.7 billion} NYC TLC taxi records on Snowflake (Bronze/Silver/Gold architecture), covering ingestion, schema unification, quality assessment, EDA, format benchmarking, external data augmentation, market analysis, and geographic visualization.

Key findings: (1) \textbf{The February 2019 FHVHV regulation} was the single largest structural event -- more impactful than COVID-19 -- immediately reorganising market reporting and crystallising Uber at $\sim$55--60\% market share. (2) \textbf{COVID-19} caused a $>$90\% drop in traditional taxi demand in April 2020; Yellow and Green recovered to only $\sim$25\% of pre-pandemic volumes, while FHVHV reached new highs by 2022. (3) \textbf{All four service types share identical temporal demand shapes} (hourly cosine $\geq$0.99, DOW cosine 1.00), confirming a uniform underlying urban mobility rhythm. (4) \textbf{Spatial distributions diverge sharply}: Yellow--Green cosine 0.08 (complementary areas); FHV--FHVHV 0.85 (overlapping citywide markets).
